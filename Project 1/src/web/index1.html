What is JDM?

Japanese Domestic Market or JDM for short, literally means “Japanese domestic market.” This term applies to Japanese-made cars. It's no secret that almost any Japanese-made car for the European market is just a “simplified” version of a car on the Japanese domestic market. Often, overseas they even have a different name. For example, the well-known Mitsubishi Lancer is a Mitsubishi Cedia, just like the Nissan Primera is a Nissan Camino. There are many such examples. Cars for the domestic market are always right-hand drive and have richer equipment. Therefore, JDM culture implies a purebred Japanese (according to some versions released before 2000).

Honda, Toyota, Mitsubishi, Nissan, Mitsuoka, Suzuki, Subaru, Isuzu are all companies that produce models for JDM. JDM is second only to the US market in production volumes. Speaking about richer equipment, we mean the mandatory presence of automatic transmission, climate control, factory xenon, richer interior trim, wood-look inserts and aluminum.

In Europe, due to left-hand traffic and a number of other reasons, a purebred Japanese is a rarity. According to this, JDM is rather a type of tuning in which the European version of a Japanese car is brought to the style of its right-hand drive counterparts. They install original JDM optics, change interior trims, and dashboards (yes, even purebred ones, in most cases they look more interesting and have more functionality). Also among fans of this culture, rare spare parts from specific famous manufacturers and tuning studios are valued: Viel Side, Erebuni, Rays, Work, Advan, SSR, BBS, Weds, Tein, TDR, Nismo, Spoon, Mugen, 5Zigen and HKS. Install Rays wheels, a Nismo body kit, and an HKS exhaust - this is style.

The JDM symbol is Domo-kun! Well, who doesn’t know or hasn’t seen this animal at least once in our time? Domo-kun is originally a fictional character from Japanese television. Cute harmless, defenseless animal.
